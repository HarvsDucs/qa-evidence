The Water Cycle

The water cycle is the continuous journey water takes as it moves between the Earth's
surface and the atmosphere. It is powered mainly by energy from the Sun and by gravity.
The same water has been cycling through this system for billions of years — the water we
drink today is the same water that dinosaurs once drank.

There are four main stages.

1. Evaporation
When the Sun heats water in oceans, lakes, and rivers, the water warms up and turns into
an invisible gas called water vapour. The vapour rises into the air. Plants add to this
through transpiration, releasing water vapour from their leaves.

2. Condensation
As water vapour rises higher, the air gets colder. The vapour cools and turns back into
tiny droplets of liquid water. Millions of these droplets gather together to form clouds.

3. Precipitation
When the water droplets in a cloud grow large and heavy enough, they fall back to Earth.
This falling water is called precipitation. Depending on the temperature, it can fall as
rain, snow, sleet, or hail.

4. Collection
The water that falls to the ground collects in oceans, lakes, and rivers, or it soaks into
the soil and becomes groundwater. From here, the Sun's heat begins the cycle all over again
with evaporation.

Why it matters
The water cycle gives us fresh water to drink and to grow crops. It shapes the weather and
the climate, and it moves water from the salty ocean to the land as clean rainwater. Because
the amount of water on Earth stays roughly the same, using and protecting water carefully
keeps this cycle healthy for people, plants, and animals.

Key terms: evaporation, transpiration, condensation, precipitation, collection, groundwater,
water vapour.
